Word Motions

Move a word at a time. The grammar's first real noun.

id: editing.word-motions · part: editing · keys: w, b, e, ge · lessons: 21, 22, 23, 24

w/b/e/ge move forward and backward by word. They're motions, so they pair with operators: dw deletes a word, cw changes one, yw yanks one.

Moving one character at a time with hjkl is fine for surgery. For everything else you want to move a word at a time. That's what w, b, e, and ge do — and because they're motions, they double as the noun half of every editing command.

Tip. Counts work: 5w jumps five words forward. So does d5w — delete five words. The grammar is consistent.
🔧 Under the Hood — What Counts as a "Word"?

Vim's notion of a word is governed by the 'iskeyword' option (:set iskeyword? to see it). By default it includes letters, digits, and _ — the things that look like an identifier in most languages.

A "word" is a maximal run of keyword characters, or a maximal run of non-keyword non-whitespace characters. So in foo->bar, the words are foo, ->, and bar. w skips between all three.

If that's too granular for you, WORD (W, B, E, gE) treats anything not whitespace as part of the word. In foo->bar, that's a single WORD. See `wider-motions.WORD`.

Tip. Mnemonic: lowercase = stops at punctuation; uppercase = whitespace only.

🔧 Under the Hood — Word motions are a token-class scanner

w, b, e, ge aren't regex-driven; they classify each character into one of three token classes — word, punctuation, whitespace — and step through class boundaries. Empty lines count as a hard boundary regardless of class. The capitalized variants (W, B, E) collapse word and punctuation into a single class, so they only stop at whitespace. The implementation is one scanner with a swappable classifier.

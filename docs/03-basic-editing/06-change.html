Change

c is delete-then-insert, atomically.

id: editing.change · part: editing · keys: c, cw, C, cc, S, s · lessons: 36, 37, 38, 39

c is the change operator. cw changes a word; cc changes a line; C changes to end of line. S and s are short forms that change a whole line and a single character respectively.

c is what d-then-i would be if you didn't have to think. It deletes the same range d would, and drops you straight into Insert mode at that spot. Then Esc closes the change as one undoable, dot-repeatable unit.

🔧 Under the Hood — Change Is Delete + Insert as One Atomic "Change"

Internally, c{motion} does three things:

1. Cuts the range covered by {motion} into the unnamed and numbered registers (just like d). 2. Switches to Insert mode at the start of the cut range. 3. Marks an undo boundary that closes when you press Esc.

Step 3 is the important one. The whole episode — delete, type, leave Insert — becomes one entry in the undo tree. u reverts all of it. . replays all of it.

That's why cw-type-something-Esc-n-. is one of the most useful patterns in Vim: jump to next match, change-the-word, repeat. Each repeat is one undoable operation, not many. See `recipes.cgn-dot` for the polished form.

Tip. Watch out: cw behaves like ce (change to end of word) — not what dw does. This is a deliberate, ancient quirk. The change operator with a word motion changes just the word, not the trailing whitespace. Most people prefer it that way.
Tip. Synonyms to memorize: C = c$. S = cc. s = cl. s doesn't get a lot of love — it's a one-character substitute, useful when you want to type a replacement immediately and skip the explicit r-then-look-around.

🔧 Under the Hood — Change is delete-then-insert, with one historical quirk

c is not a primitive: it runs the delete operator, then enters insert mode at the landing position. The famous cw = ce quirk falls out of this — change adjusts the motion's endpoint to drop the trailing whitespace so the inserted text doesn't push it. Knowing change is a composition lets you predict its behavior with any motion you've already learned.

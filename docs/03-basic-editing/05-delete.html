Delete

d is the verb. Pair it with any motion.

id: editing.delete · part: editing · keys: x, X, d, dw, D, dd · lessons: 31, 32, 33, 34, 35

d is the delete operator. It takes a motion: dw deletes a word, d$ deletes to end of line. Doubling the operator (dd) makes it linewise. x and X are convenience deletes for single characters.

d is the delete operator. It's a verb that needs a noun. The noun is any motion or text object. dw deletes a word. d$ deletes to end of line. dip deletes the inner paragraph. The grammar carries you here.

Tip. Counts compose multiplicatively. 3d2w deletes six words. The 3 multiplies the 2 multiplies the motion. Read it as "three times: delete two words."
🔧 Under the Hood — Delete Doesn't Delete — It Moves to a Register

When you press dw, Vim cuts the deleted text and stores it in two registers:

1. The unnamed register ("") — your most-recent yank-or-delete. 2. A numbered register ("1–"9) — a rolling buffer of the last nine deletes.

So p after dw pastes the word back. "1p pastes the most-recent delete. "2p the one before. Delete is just "cut." This is also why deleting and then pasting elsewhere is the standard Vim move-text idiom.

The one register not touched by delete is the yank register ("0), which only updates on actual yanks. So if you yank, then delete, then paste, you get the delete. Use "0p to get the yank back. Full treatment: `registers.numbered` and `registers.unnamed`.

Tip. D = d$. dd (linewise delete) is the doubled-operator form. Every operator follows that pattern: a doubled operator means "act on this whole line." cc, yy, >>, ==, etc.
Tip. Curiosity: x and X are essentially dl and dh respectively. They exist for ergonomics — single-character deletes happen so often that they get one-key shortcuts.

Reference

Key Action
x Delete char under cursor
X Delete char before cursor
d{motion} Delete what {motion} covers
dd Delete current line (linewise)
D Delete to end of line (= d$)
diw Delete inner word
dap Delete around paragraph
{n}dd Delete {n} lines

Worked example — x dw dd

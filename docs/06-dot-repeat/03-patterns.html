Dot Patterns

Search + change + dot. The two main refactor idioms.

id: dot.patterns · part: dot · keys: *, cw, n, ., cgn · lessons: 73

The two best refactor patterns in Vim both pair search with the dot command. *cw…<Esc>n. is the classic; cgn. is the smoother modern version that lets you skip without thinking.

Two idioms make Vim feel like a refactoring tool. Both compose search with the dot command. They look almost identical, but the second one is much more pleasant to use.

The classic: *cw…<Esc>n.

Cursor on foo. * jumps to the next foo and stashes it as the search pattern. cw changes the word. Type the replacement. Esc. Now n jumps to the next match; . repeats the change. n . n . … walks you through, with the option to skip with n alone.

The smoother: cgn.

Same setup, but use cgn instead of cw. The gn motion means "the next search match." So cgn = change the next match. After the first one, you can just press . repeatedly — each . jumps and changes. Skip an occurrence by pressing n once before ..

🔧 Under the Hood — Why cgn. Is Better

gn's genius is that it bakes the jump into the motion. So cgn doesn't need a separate n between repetitions — the motion finds the next match and selects it as part of the change.

Result: pure . . . repeats. Mental load goes from two-key cadence (n. n.) to one-key cadence (. .).

The analogous yank/delete forms exist: ygn, dgn. But change is the common one because refactor = change.

gN is the same idea but for the previous match, in case you want to walk backward.

Tip. These patterns are why . is called "the most important key in Vim." Combined with * and cgn, every grep-and-replace becomes a guided walk: you can preview each change, undo with u, skip with n, accept with ..

Reference

Sequence What happens
*cw…Escn. Classic: change-then-walk with n.
*cgn…Esc. Smooth: change next match, then pure . repeats

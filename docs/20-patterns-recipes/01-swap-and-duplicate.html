Swap and Duplicate Lines

ddp swaps two lines. yyp duplicates one.

id: patterns.swap-and-duplicate · part: patterns-recipes · keys: dd, p, yy · lessons: 461, 462

Two of the oldest Vim recipes: ddp moves the current line down past the next one (a swap); yyp duplicates the current line. Both compose primitives you already know.

Two recipes you'll see in every "Vim tricks" list. They aren't features — they're compositions of primitives that happen to do something useful.

Recipe Effect
ddp Swap current line with the one below
ddkP Swap current line with the one above
yyp Duplicate current line below
xp Swap two characters
🔧 Under the Hood — Why It Works

dd cuts the current line into the unnamed register. The cursor lands on what was the next line. p pastes the line back — but p pastes a line-wise register below the cursor's line, putting the cut line one position later than where it started. Net effect: swap.

yy yanks the line; the cursor doesn't move. p pastes below. Net effect: duplicate.

xp: x deletes the character under the cursor (into unnamed reg) and the cursor moves to what was the next char. p pastes a character-wise register after the cursor — placing the cut character one position later. Swap.

Tip. Move multiple lines? :m is cleaner: :m+1 moves current line down one. Visual + :m moves a block.

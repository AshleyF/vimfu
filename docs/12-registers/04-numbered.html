Numbered Registers

"0 = last yank. "1–"9 = the last nine deletes.

id: registers.numbered · part: registers · keys: "0, "1, "9 · lessons: 122

"0 holds your most recent yank; it never updates on delete. "1 holds your most recent multi-character delete; "2 the one before, and so on through "9. They shift on every delete.

Vim quietly keeps a backlog of your recent yanks and deletes. "0 is your most recent yank. "1 through "9 are the last nine deletes — they shift down on each new one.

🔧 Under the Hood — The Two Different Update Rules

"0 — only updated on yank. Stays untouched by delete and change. So if you yank, then delete, then paste, you get the delete. To get your yank back: "0p.

"1–"9 — a sliding window of the last nine deletes (and changes), not yanks. On each new delete:

  • Old "9 falls off.
  • "8 → "9.
  • "7 → "8. … "1 → "2. New delete → "1.

Exception: small charwise deletes (less than a line, no newline) go into "-, the small-delete register, and don't shift the numbered registers. So x doesn't bump anything off — only multi-character or line deletes do.

With both rules in mind, the entire register layout becomes navigable: you can paste the most recent yank, the most recent delete, or the delete before that — all by name.

🔧 Under the Hood — The . Trick (Numbered Register + Dot)

Combine numbered registers with the dot command and you get a power move:

1. Delete a line with dd. It goes to "1. 2. Move somewhere else. 3. "1p pastes the deleted line. 4. . repeats — but here's the surprise: . increments the numbered-register reference. So the next . pastes from "2, then "3, etc.

This lets you walk back through your delete history one paste at a time. See `recipes.numbered-paste`.

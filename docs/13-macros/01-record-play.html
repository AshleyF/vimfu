Recording and Playing Macros

qa records into register a. q stops. @a plays.

id: macros.record-play · part: macros · keys: qa, q, @a, @@ · lessons: 126, 127, 128, 129

qa starts recording into register a; press q again to stop. @a plays back. @@ replays the most recent macro.

Macros are Vim's loop. Record a sequence of keystrokes once; replay it with one key. They're how you turn 100 lines of repetitive editing into 4 keystrokes.

Tip. While recording, the status line shows recording @a. Don't worry — the recording is just keystrokes-into-a-register; nothing is locked, nothing is precious. If you mess up, press q to stop, then qa to start fresh.
🔧 Under the Hood — How Recording Works

qa: Vim opens register a for writing and tags every subsequent keypress to be appended to it. Each key still does its normal job — you're editing as usual.

q: Closes the register. The recorded keystrokes (including all the Esc, motion, and operator keys) sit in "a as a string.

@a: Reads "a and feeds the contents back into Vim's input buffer. They run as if you typed them. Errors abort playback.

This is just type-then-replay. There's no "macro language." Anything you can type, you can record.

🔧 Under the Hood — Idiom: Make Your Macro Idempotent

A reliable macro starts with a stable anchor — usually 0 (start of line) or a search. That way, no matter where the cursor is, the macro begins at a known position.

It usually ends with a forward step — typically j or n — so successive plays move down through the buffer.

Example: "add a semicolon to end of every line." Record: qa, A;Esc, j, 0, q. Now 100@a adds semicolons to 100 lines.

If the macro fails (e.g. no more lines, or no match for a search), playback aborts. That's why "do it 100 times" is safe — it stops when there's nothing left to do.

Worked example — qa ... q then @a

Record once, play forever.

🔧 Under the Hood — Macros are input virtualization

Recording a macro stores the literal keystroke sequence into a register. Playback feeds those keystrokes back through the same input pipeline a real keyboard would feed. Every subsystem — operators, search, marks, insert — must remain callable from synthetic input. That's why macros stop on the first command that would have rung the bell: the input simulator treats a failed command as a real failed command and aborts the stream.

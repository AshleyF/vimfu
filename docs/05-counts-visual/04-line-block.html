Line and Block Visual

V selects whole lines. Ctrl-V selects rectangles.

id: counts-visual.line-block · part: counts-visual · keys: V, Ctrl-V · lessons: 65, 66, 67

V enters linewise Visual: motions extend the selection by whole lines. Ctrl-V enters blockwise Visual: the selection is a rectangle, useful for column edits.

v selects characters. Two more visual modes select differently-shaped regions: V for whole lines, Ctrl-V for rectangles.

Tip. From any Visual mode, press v, V, or Ctrl-V to switch to that mode while keeping the selection.
🔧 Under the Hood — Three Modes, Three Selection Shapes

Internally Vim represents selections as (anchor, cursor, type) where type is char, line, or block. The type changes how operators behave:

  • Charwise (v) — operators see a contiguous span of bytes from anchor to cursor.
  • Linewise (V) — operators see whole lines containing anchor and cursor. Column position doesn't matter.
  • Blockwise (Ctrl-V) — operators see a rectangle. Yanks come back blockwise, paste back as rectangles, and I / A work on every line of the block (see `visual.block-insert-append`).
🔧 Under the Hood — Block Mode Is the Killer Feature for Columns

Block-visual on a column lets you insert, append, change, or delete down a column across many lines. Common uses:

  • Add // to many lines: Ctrl-V-select-down-I//SpaceEsc. The prefix is duplicated to every line of the block.
  • Replace a column of values: Ctrl-V-select-rectangle-c-type-replacement-Esc.
  • Delete a column of leading whitespace: Ctrl-V-select-the-leading-spaces-d.

The I/A-replicates-to-every-line trick happens only in block mode and only with capital I / A (not lowercase i / a).

🔧 Under the Hood — Visual-line mode normalizes to whole rows

Linewise visual (V) doesn't extend the charwise range to line ends — it replaces both endpoints with (start_of_line, end_of_line) every frame. That normalization happens before any operator sees the selection, so paste, delete, indent, and join all get a clean row-span. It's also why moving horizontally in V mode doesn't appear to do anything: the normalization snaps it back.

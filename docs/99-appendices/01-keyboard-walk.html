Keyboard Walk

Every normal-mode key, organized by physical key location.

A row-by-row tour of the QWERTY keyboard: every unshifted key, every shifted key, with a note on Ctrl- variants. Useful as a wall poster and as a study plan.

Vim's bindings cover every key on the keyboard. Not most of them. Not the popular ones. Every key. Hold a finger over almost any character on a QWERTY keyboard, hit it in normal mode, and something happens — a motion, an operator, a prefix into a family of commands, a jump, an action. The empty key is the exception, not the rule.

That's also a study plan. You don't have to learn Vim's grammar all at once — you can walk the keyboard. Pick a key a day, hit it in normal mode, see what it does, look it up. After a few weeks the unshifted letters are second nature. Then shifted letters. Then shifted symbols. Then a handful of useful Ctrl- variants. By the time you've finished one full lap of the keyboard, you've absorbed essentially every common normal-mode command in Vim.

The order of importance: 1. Unshifted keys — the bulk of day-to-day editing. Motions (hjkl, w, b, e, f, t), operators (d, c, y), modes (i, a, o, v, :), basic editing (u, x, p, r, s). 2. Shifted keys — bigger, opposite, or specialised siblings of the unshifted versions. A appends at end of line, O opens above, I inserts at first non-blank, N reverses search, G jumps to last line. 3. Shifted symbols — search-word-under-cursor (*, #), sentence motions ((, )), paragraph motions ({, }), indent operators (>, <), end-of-line ($), bracket-match (%). 4. Ctrl- variants — window prefixes (Ctrl-W…), scrolling (Ctrl-D, Ctrl-U, Ctrl-F, Ctrl-B), the jumplist (Ctrl-O, Ctrl-I), inc/dec (Ctrl-A, Ctrl-X), redo (Ctrl-R), file-info (Ctrl-G). A full reference of every Ctrl- key lives in Complete Key Reference.

Tip. If you're just starting out: walk the unshifted keys first, in any order. hjkl obviously, but also pick the strange ones — t, s, q, m, z. Most of them either do something useful immediately or are prefixes into a family worth learning.

Pass 2 — Shifted keys

Once the unshifted keys feel automatic, walk the keyboard again with shift held. The pattern: shifted-letter = bigger / opposite / line-flavored version of its unshifted counterpart. Shifted-number = a punctuation symbol with its own meaning. Shifted-punctuation = often a paired motion or an operator.

Key Function

Pass 3 — Ctrl- variants

Most letters also have a Ctrl- variant. Many of them are useful, a handful are essential, and a few do nothing in normal mode at all. The most worthwhile to learn:

Key Function
Ctrl-W Window prefix (Ctrl-Wh/j/k/l navigate, Ctrl-Ws/v split, Ctrl-Wc close, …)
Ctrl-R Redo (undo of undo)
Ctrl-D Scroll half-page down
Ctrl-U Scroll half-page up
Ctrl-F Scroll full page forward
Ctrl-B Scroll full page backward
Ctrl-E Scroll one line down (cursor stays)
Ctrl-Y Scroll one line up (cursor stays)
Ctrl-O Jumplist backward — return to previous cursor location
Ctrl-I Jumplist forward (same key as Tab)
Ctrl-A Increment number under or after cursor
Ctrl-X Decrement number under or after cursor
Ctrl-G Show file info (path, line number, total lines)
Ctrl-] Jump to tag under cursor (or LSP definition with plugin)
Ctrl-T Jump back from tag
Ctrl-V Visual-block mode (column selection)

Every other Ctrl- combination — including the obscure and the rarely-useful — is catalogued in Complete Key Reference. When in doubt about what Ctrl-X, say, does in some context, look it up there.

Tip. A few Ctrl- keys are stolen by the terminal or window manager before Vim sees them: Ctrl-S (XON flow control), Ctrl-Q (XOFF), Ctrl-Z (suspend), Ctrl-C (interrupt). If a mapping doesn't fire, that's the first thing to check.

Move with hjkl

Left, down, up, right — without leaving home row

id: survival.hjkl · part: survival · keys: h, j, k, l, Left, Down, Up, Right · lessons: 13, 14

h j k l move the cursor one cell at a time. Hands stay on the home row. Arrow keys also work but defeat the purpose.

Four keys to move the cursor one cell at a time:

Key Direction Mnemonic
h Left h is on the left of the home row
j Down j has a hook that points down
k Up k is right next to j; the one above
l Right l is the last of the four

Counts work: 5j moves down five lines, 10l moves right ten characters.

Why not arrow keys?

The arrow keys also work. Vim accepts them. But they live a few inches away from the home row. Every time your fingers leave home row, you spend a beat finding it again. Over a day of editing, those beats add up.

More importantly, hjkl are right under your fingers when you are about to issue a command. dj deletes a line down. y5k yanks five lines up. The cursor keys can't combine like this — they aren't part of the grammar.

Tip. Some Vimmers turn off the arrow keys to break the habit. You don't have to be that strict. Just notice when you reach for them; that's when hjkl would be faster.
🔧 Under the Hood — Why hjkl?

Bill Joy wrote vi on an ADM-3A terminal at UC Berkeley in 1976. The ADM-3A keyboard had no dedicated arrow keys — instead, the h, j, k, l keys were physically labeled with left/down/up/right arrows. Joy reused the existing terminal control codes those keys produced. The ADM-3A is long dead; the convention lives.

It turns out to be a real ergonomic win: those four keys are on the home row of the dominant hand for most touch-typists. If Joy had been working on a different terminal — one with arrow keys somewhere else — modern Vim would feel different. As it stands, the right hand never leaves the home row to navigate.

🔧 Under the Hood — Sticky Column

When you move k or j up and down, the cursor tries to stay in the same column — even across lines that are too short to reach that column. If you're at column 30 and you press j onto a line of length 10, the cursor lands at column 10 (the end). Press j again onto a line of length 50: the cursor returns to column 30, not column 10. Vim remembers your intended column.

Horizontal motions (h, l, w, f, etc.) reset the sticky column. So a right-moving motion in the middle of vertical navigation will cost you the original column.

This is the cause of the frequent "I pressed j a few times and the cursor walked left" surprise — you crossed a short line. The cursor is following the rules; the rules just don't match terminal-emulator intuition.

Reference

Key Action
h One character left
j One line down
k One line up
l One character right
{n}j n lines down (etc.)
Backspace One character left, wrapping to previous line at column 0
Space One character right, wrapping to next line at end of line
gj, gk By visual line — useful when wrap is on (deeper dive in wider-motions.line-start)
